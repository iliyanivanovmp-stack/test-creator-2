With the burger analogy in mind, here is how Idefine a website:

Awebsite is a domain that visually presents an Offer and communicates (through Copywriting and Creative Assets) the Unique Value of your products or services compared to competitors. This is what Icall The Five Pillars of Websites. These break down into a simple list:

1. Offer: The unique products and how you’re selling them

2. Unique Value Propositions: The reasons why visitors should choose you over competitors

3. Copywriting: Communication supporting Value Propositions through text

4. Creative: Images and videos

5. UI/UX: The visual representation of the four previous pillars on your site. i.e., the customer journey It’s easy to overthink your website. There are so many options for layout, colors, buttons, features, etc. At the end of the day, all the elements of your website fall under one of the Five Pillars. That means there are only five aspects to focus on, which simplifies optimizing websites. These five pillars will be the framework for taking action from this book. Let’s dig a little deeper into the Five Pillars of Websites, using our burger stand example again:

THE OFFER The Offer is what you’re selling to the visitor. It’ s the burger, software, or service that you have spent years perfecting. The Offer is more than just the main product or service. It’s the entire package. If you sell burgers, you probably also sell fries, drinks, and other sides. All of these elements together add up to the Offer. If you’re a SaaS or Ecommerce site, your Offer may include a free trial, a free product with your main purchase, or free shipping on orders over a specific number. The Offer is more than just the product or service you’re selling. How you shape and present the Offer is one of the biggest levers in any business. Amajor component of that presentation is Value Propositions. UNIQUE VALUE PROPOSITIONS Unique Value Propositions are the reasons why visitors should choose your product or service over competitors. Just like the name, Unique Value Propositions (UVPs) are unique reasons to buy your Offer. No competitor should be able to claim the same Value Propositions (or combination of Value Propositions). Some marketers call these “angles” or “differentiators” to explain why customers should buy from you and not others. But being better is not enough. Your product may have ten impressive advantages over your competitors, but you have to know and use the UVPs your customers care about most. If you choose the wrong UVPs to focus on, your perfect customers may not be interested.

For example, a supplement may be gluten-free, low-sugar, FDAapproved, and vegan. Which of these things do your ideal customers care about most? If customers truly care about low-sugar supplements but you’re focusing on being an FDA-approved supplement, fewer customers will purchase from you. How? With Copywriting and Creative. COPYWRITING AND CREATIVE Copywriting and Creative are how you communicate the unique values of your product or service. This is the picture of the delicious, juicy cheeseburger on your menu and the luscious, gooey, savory words you use to describe it. Copywriting is the text on your website that specifically supports your UVPs and reasons to take The Offer. Many marketers think UVPs are the same as Copywriting, but they are separate pillars. Your UVPs are the “why” of your website, and Copywriting (along with Creative) is the “how. ” You may have just 3 UVPs, but there are infinite ways to express them through words and visuals. Copywriting goes far deeper than paragraphs of text. It includes everything from button text to even the names of your products. Every word on your website must be intentional and give the highest chance of the visitor accepting your Offer.

Creative in this framework is the images and videos you use on your website to present The Offer. USER INTERFACE AND USER EXPERIENCE User Interface (UI) is how your Offer, UVPs, Copywriting, and Creatives are structured and presented visually on your website. When you think “website design,” you are mostly thinking about UI. User Experience (UX) is “how” visitors take you up on your Offer. It’ s the customer journey from being a visitor to buying a product. Think of the User Experience as the service your burger stand provides to customers. W E CAN T ALK ALL day about Conversion Rate, Average Order Value, and Revenue Per Session, but none of that matt ers unless you understand how the conscious and subconscious mind works. Websites that perform best cater to the subconscious mind—and here’ s why. Your subconscious mind is a machine. According to the Association for Psychological Science (2013), your mind processes up to elevenp million bits of information every second. A “bit” is the smallest piece of information your brain processes, like a facial expression, a color, a word, and subtle cues from your environment that could spell danger or opportunity. By comparison, your conscious mind—the part you actively use—handles just forty bits per second, as psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi explains replace with: “in his seminal work on flow states. ” THE GATOR AND THE JUDGE

That’s a mind-boggling 250,000x difference between the conscious and subconscious! While your subconscious scans and processes massive amounts of data quickly and automatically, your conscious mind can only process a trickle of information. This gap leads to information overload. If your website forces users to rely on their conscious minds to figure out what’s happening, they’ll get overwhelmed, frustrated, and leave. Nobel Prize winner Herbert Simon put it best, way back in 1971: “ Awealth of information creates a poverty of attention” . For websites, this isn’t just a fun psychological concept—it’s a revenue killer. Your subconscious brain works fast, scanning for simple answers: What’s this about? Is it for me? Can Itrust it? These questions are answered in seconds without users realizing it. If your site isn’t clear and easy to understand, visitors leave without a second thought. Designing for the subconscious isn’t just about making a site look nice—it’s about understanding who is actually scanning and reading it. Websites that are clear, simple, and resonant with visitors make more sales every time. This idea is the foundation for creating websites that print money. It sounds complicated, but all of buyer psychology can be explained with my super simple-to-understand framework, The Gator and The Judge, which illustrates how the subconscious and conscious mind interact with your website. Long story short: The goal of your website is to please The Gator and avoid The Judge.

THE GA TOR AND THE JUDGE The Gator is your subconscious, primal instinct and behavior. It’s feeling like you’re on autopilot and doing or consuming things without intense analysis. The Judge is that conscious, analytical, and critically thinking brain. The Gator and Judge are also known as System 1 and System 2 thinking. These terms were made famous by Nobel Prize–winning behavioral economist Daniel Kahneman. Iprefer the terms Gator and Judge because they’re more descriptive: The Gator is instinctive and hungry. It wants to move fast and satisfy its hunger. The Judge wants to slow things down, deliberate, and, well, judge every decision. If you pick up a cup of coffee, are you thinking critically about how you should put your hand around it? No! After picking up a cup thousands of times, you don’t need to think consciously about it, meaning The Gator is picking up the cup. When you walk down the street, are you thinking of where to place your feet every step? Hopefully not! While The Gator helps us do many things that keep us alive, it can also give into distraction and emotion. That’s not a bad thing, but as a marketer, you need to be aware of it because The Gator is your key to increasing sales. The Gator’s decision-making process is simple. It acts on instinct: ▶ Is this what Iwant? ▶ Is this easy? ▶ Is this familiar? ▶ Is this something Ido without thinking?

If the answer to these questions is “yes,” The Gator eats! But if The Gator has to pause and second-guess the answer, it stops. Now, here is how to appeal to The Gator: You need to optimize your website’s five pillars (Offer, UVP , Copy, Creative, and UI/UX) to make your products and services desirable, safe, and familiar. You don’t want The Gator to think about what it should do . . . You just want it to say, “Oooo, me likey,” and make a purchase. This implies you have products The Gator wants, an Offer it can’t refuse, non-negotiable UVPs, an intuitive buying experience, and Copywriting/Creative that resonates with it. Why do you think stores put candy and cheap products along the checkout line? They know The Gator will get bored and make an impulse decision to grab a cheap product. The Gator takes the path of least resistance at all times, and it’s all instinctual. The Gator is your brain on autopilot. You may think you are in control of your own decisions, but 95 percent of the time, The Gator is steering the ship. This has been backed by countless psychological studies dating back to the 1970s. The Judge is called on when there’s distrust, irritation, skepticism, or any negative subconscious emotion, no matter how small. If something on your website turns The Gator off—a confusing UX, untrustworthy testimonials, or the “wrong” image choice—it will summon The Judge to investigate further. And more often than not, that will cause you to lose the sale. Here’s an example (one I’ll use throughout the book): Imagine a new shoe company that sells mesh-based sneakers competing with Adidas, Nike, and New Balance. The company’s first product is a pair of sneakers designed for runners whose top priority is injury prevention.

THE GA TOR AND THE JUDGE Now imagine a new visitor, after hearing about the brand from a friend, visits the website. Instead of an image of a pain-free runner in the Hero section, the visitor sees someone dunking a basketball. This visitor’s Gator will immediately feel this sneaker isn’t the right one for them. Either they are on the wrong website, or they are not the right customer. Even if everything else about the website is designed for runners who want to live pain-free, this poorly chosen image will summon The Judge, making the sale much more diffi cult. RPS is great for comparing “apples to apples,” which are called “elements of a kind. ” Elements of a kind are comparing Product Tiles to other Product Tiles or FAQ to other FAQs. This is why the Headline and Hero Image are so important. One hundred percent of users will see your Hero Image and header. If you watch screen recordings or use scrollmaps and see that users are leaving your page without even scrolling, it means your hero section is ineffective. If the Hero section doesn’t make them want to learn more (i.e., scroll down the page), they’ll bounce from the page, and you lose the sale instantly. Many marketers write their headlines as descriptions of the product, but this is actually the wrong approach. Headlines are supposed to tell your customers the end result of using your product, or what Ilike to call The Dream Outcome. It’s the sub-headline’s job is to describe the product. Subheadlines are how your product gets them their Dream Outcome. That’s why Icall subheadlines The Vessel. Combined, your headlines paint a picture of The Dream Outcome for your customers, then The Vessel to help them reach it. Customer feedback surveys are the number one way to determine your customers’ Dream Outcomes. Listen to their answers on why they chose your product over competitors and what problem they need solving. Often, customers will describe their dream outcome perfectly—all you have to do is copy it to your headline!

Let’s go back to the example of a mesh-based sneaker brand making shoes for runners whose top priority is to prevent injury. Imagine you get the results back from the Customer Feedback Surveys, and you discover that 70 percent of your customers are battling a chronic injury like joint pain or shin splits. In addition, over 50 percent are currently training for a long-distance race. The Dream Outcome for your customers is crystal clear: To finish the race without pain or maybe even to set a personal best time! Your sneakers are perfectly built to help runners achieve this dream outcome, especially because of the mesh technology that supports your heel and eases joint pain. But that’s not why customers buy. Alot of marketers would make the mistake of writing a headline: “The most advanced running shoe ever made—with patented mesh technology for support. ” This is not an effective Headline. It says nothing about what runners want: to stay healthy. Mentioning the mesh technology may work for a small number of highly educated athletes, but it will not appeal to most runners. Acquiring massive amounts of new customers will be much more expensive with this headline. Amore effective headline is one that advertises the dream outcome for your customers: “Running Shoes That Keep You Healthy and Happy.” Then the subheadline should highlight The Vessel, or how customers will achieve their dream: “The #1 running shoe for athletes with chronic joint pain— recommended by doctors and trusted by thousands.” If your Headlines don’t feed The Gator what it wants, the rest of your Landing Page doesn’t matter, as they’ll just leave the page and not

scroll down or buy. Use your top real estate to highlight the number one thing your customers want, then show them how to get it. Notice Ididn’t mention the mesh technology in the second Headline—that detail can be shared later down the Landing Page once customers are in “research mode. ” First though, you need to get them interested in learning more. The best Headlines speak directly to a customer’s deepest desires, which is why the BDF data is so important. As always, ask your cus - tomers what they want, give them what they want, and you’ll get what you want, which is more money. Every single type of Landing Page starts with a Headline, so use these principles across all of your pages. In reality, this does the opposite. Users do not like the feeling of hunting down a price. The Judge awakens! Users behave differently when buying $20 products vs. $2,000 products. There’s no problem with your product being expensive, but The Gator shops differently when it’s a higher price. Bundles are typically priced with a “discount” built-in, meaning customers pay less for the bundle than if they bought the products separately. This adds to the “great deal” you’re offering—they are getting their dream outcome for less! Customers get what they want, and you get what you want, which is a higher AOV . HOW TO ACTUALLY MEASURE WEBSITE PERFORMANCE Let’s go back to the three websites with Conversion Rates of 1.2 percent, 2.3 percent, and 7.4 percent.

REVENUE PER SESSION, THE NORTH STAR OF CRO. Revenue Per Session (RPS) is the total revenue from your website divided by the number of visitors on your website. If 1,000 visitors buy $5,000 of product, your RPS is $5.00. In other words, RPS combines your Average Order Value and Conversion Rate into a single metric. It gives you a clearer picture of your website and marketing efficiency while accounting for both revenue and your website’s ability to convert customers. (For sales-led companies, this metric is Lead Per Session [LPS], which follows all the same principles as RPS). If Itell you three businesses have an RPS of $2.35, $3.26, and $6.74, we can confidently say the website with the $6.74 RPS is the most profitable.

 RPS is not a silver bullet on its own either, but it’s a far better indicator of website performance than Conversion Rate. It allows you to compare companies“apples to apples vs. apples to oranges. If you have five categories of products on your website and each has its own Collections Page, you can compare the RPS of each Collections Page to determine which category is most efficient at driving revenue (but not necessarily the most total revenue). This helps you determine if you should send more visitors to that page and what pages to focus on. Another useful application for RPS is evaluating the performance of Landing Pages. To measure the effectiveness of a Landing Page, you need to add the cost per click (CPC) plus the cost to fulfill each order and then compare that to your RPS. There’s much more to this, but I’m sure you get the point. I’d wager 99 percent of marketers will look at their ROAS or cost-perlead on each Landing Page to determine the best-performing Landing Page, but look instead at the RPS or LPS of these Landing Pages. This will help you make much better business decisions in your advertising, email, SMS, SEO, and CRO campaigns (every marketing channel). But remember, even RPS can be misleading if not taken in context. Landing Pages with higher paid traffic will naturally have a lower RPS than other pages because you’re sending cold traffic—users discovering your business for the first time—to them. Those first-time visitors are 25 percent as likely to buy than returning, second-time (or more) visitors.

PART 4: MEASUREMENT Split testing is useless if we can’t measure the impact of the changes. The most important things here are assuring you have enough data to conclude a test and having the confidence that the revenue lift from the test will continue once you implement it on your website. There are a few rules you must follow, and I’ll explain each: ▶ Run tests for a minimum of two weeks. ▶ Reach Statistical Significance and Statistical Power. ▶ Look at metrics other than revenue. Run Tests for a Minimum of Two Weeks It doesn’t matter how much traffic your site receives—running tests for a minimum of two weeks is non-negotiable. Iexplained why in Chapter 14 but it’s worth reiterating: It’s not just about the volume of traffic during a test but also the variety of traffic. There are too many variables outside your control to draw conclusions from tests that last less than two weeks. What if there’s a hurricane that hits Florida during your test, and 8 percent of your web traffic comes from Florida? That could completely skew your results! Not to mention the difference in shopping habits during the day vs. night and on weekends vs. weekdays. Be patient, Padawan. Let your tests simmer for a full two weeks or more. Let’s move on.

Statistical Significance and Statistical Power In addition to volume and variety of traffic, you need to be sure your tests reach Statistical Significance and Statistical Power to be conclusive. Ipromise you don’t need a degree in data analysis to grasp these concepts. In the simplest terms, Statistical Significance is the probability your test is accurate. Statistical Power is the repeatability of your test. If you run it one thousand times, how many times would it get the same results? Statistical Significance and Power are largely determined by two factors: Volume: The more data you have, the easier it is to reach Statistical Significance and Power. Impact: The larger the revenue (or lead/opt-in) lift com - pared to the control, the more likely you are to achieve Statistical Significance and Power. You do not have to calculate Statistical Significance and Power by hand. Many split testing platforms run these calculations for you. If not, there are free calculators on the internet to help. Statistical Significance and Power are measured as percentages up to 99.999 percent, never 100 percent. The higher the percentage, the higher your confidence in the test.

The CRO industry “standard” is to achieve 95 percent or greater Statistical Significance and 80 percent or greater Statistical Power. Iput “standard” in quotes because while this is a good target, it is not a concrete rule. There are some caveats to these thresholds that I’ll cover later. First, let me show you why having enough data is very important. Here’s an example of a test with 10,000 visitors in each variant of the test. Variant Bsaw a 10 percent increase in conversions: Variant A: 10,000 visitors, 200 purchases Variant B: 10,000 visitors, 220 purchases Aft er plugging the results into a Statistical Significance calculator, you can see that the chance of Variant Boutperforming Variant Ais 83.9 percent. Not good enough by industry standards. You have two options here:

1. Cut the test and deem it inconclusive

2. Let the test run longer to see if Statistical Significance improves

What would you do? If it were me, I’d let this test run every time because it shows a ton of potential. A 10 percent increase in conversion is a home -run optimization, but we need to be sure the results will stick. If this was the two-week mark for this test, I’d likely give it another week of data to see if it can break the 95 percent Significance mark. I’d also look at secondary metrics like Add to Carts, Click-Through Rates, and “soft metrics” to see the impact this test had downstream. Let’s say you let the test run for another week, and you double the t r a ffic .Variant Bis still showing a 10 percent lift in conversions. Variant A: 20,000 visitors, 400 purchases Variant B: 20,000 visitors, 440 purchases The Confidence Level of this test is 92 percent. It still hasn’t reached the 95 percent industry threshold, so what do you do? This is where I’d look closely at the secondary metrics of AOV , RPS, and Conversion Rate (CVR). If all those metrics saw positive lift s, Iwould implement this change (I’ll discuss secondary metrics
