# Canopy CRO Research Brief

**Data Sources:** Meta Ads, Google Ads Transparency, Reviews & UGC (Amazon), Page Speed Insights, Site Screenshots

---

## Insights

The showerhead PDP, which receives all of Canopy's Meta paid traffic and Google Search ad traffic, scores 11 out of 100 on mobile. LCP is 15.6 seconds against a 2.5-second target. TBT is 3,840ms against a 200ms threshold. Research shows every 1-second delay in mobile load time reduces conversions by approximately 7%. Every dollar Canopy spends on Meta and Google lands on a page most mobile users abandon before it fully loads.

The buy box on the same page compounds the problem. There are three stacked purchase options: a pre-selected subscription widget with a nested one-time vs. every-90-days comparison table inside it, followed by two more radio buttons below for a subscription-without-aroma option at $135 and a one-time option at $150. CLS of 0.285 means the buy box is physically shifting as it loads on mobile, disrupting click intent. Meta Ad 2 (running since March 2025) leads with "Try Canopy Risk-Free" as its CTA. On the landing page, the 60-day return policy is in small grey text below the ATC button, not the prominent reassurance the ad creates.

Amazon reviews reveal exactly why customers buy, and neither the PDP nor the homepage reflects it. Hard water relief is the stated reason for purchase in the majority of reviews, cited by buyers across Texas, Florida, Arizona, and New York City. "Noticed after first use" or "noticed after first wash" appears across dozens of reviews. The PDP headline is the generic "Handheld Filtered Showerhead." The homepage hero leads with "Buy one water filter, get one $25 off" over a dark image where no single product is clearly visible. Google Search ads claim "Over 7,000 5-Star Reviews." That number appears nowhere on the homepage or PDP above the fold.

The cart has no recovery path for customers who bypass the subscription option at the PDP. Once a buyer adds the showerhead at $150 one-time, there is no prompt to switch to the $125 subscription before checkout. The cross-sell section also recommends the Handheld Filtered Showerhead at $150, which is already in the cart.

Conservative revenue math across the three test pages: Showerhead PDP (est. 25,000 sessions/month) at a 0.5% CR lift equals +$16,500/month. Homepage (est. 60,000 sessions/month) at a 0.25% CR lift equals +$19,800/month. Cart subscription conversion at a 15% switch rate for one-time buyers equals +$42,000/year in new recurring subscription revenue.

## Slot 1: Showerhead PDP Buy Box Simplification

**Type:** A/B test (1 variation vs. control)
**Page:** Handheld Filtered Showerhead PDP (getcanopy.co/products/handheld-filtered-showerhead)
**Revenue potential:** Est. 25,000 PDP sessions/month x 0.5% CR lift x $132 blended AOV (65% sub at $125, 35% one-time at $150) = +$16,500/month. Sessions estimated from Meta campaign running since March 2025, Google Search, and organic traffic.

**Hypothesis:** If we replace the current 3-option nested buy box with a clean two-tab toggle between Subscribe & Save ($125) and One-Time ($150), and move the 60-day risk-free return policy directly below the ATC button, add-to-cart rate will increase because the current structure creates decision paralysis and CLS of 0.285 causes the buy box to shift as it loads on mobile.

**Data:** The buy box currently has three stacked purchase options: a pre-selected subscription widget with an internal one-time vs. every-90-days comparison table inside it, a subscription-without-aroma option at $135, and a one-time-only option at $150. This requires a customer to parse three tiers of pricing before adding to cart (Source: Site Screenshots). CLS of 0.285 on the mobile PDP places it in the "Poor" range above the 0.25 threshold, indicating buy box elements are shifting as the page loads and disrupting interaction (Source: Page Speed). Meta Ad 2 (running since March 2025) uses "Try Canopy Risk-Free" as its CTA, but the 60-day return policy is in small grey text in a trust bar below the ATC button, not presented as the risk-removal signal the ad implies (Source: Meta Ads, Site Screenshots). Research shows simplifying a buy box's subscription toggle lifted reorders by 19% (Source: CRO Community Research).

**V1:** Replace all three current buy box options with a two-tab toggle at the top of the buy box. The left tab reads "Subscribe & Save" and is selected by default. It shows "$125" in large text with "$150" struck through beside it, and one descriptive line below: "Filters + Aroma Kit delivered every 90 days." The right tab reads "One-Time" and shows "$150" in large text with no additional line. When a tab is selected, its border turns dark and its background lightens. Unselected tabs remain light grey. Below the tabs, the ATC button is full-width and updates its price label based on selection: "$125 - Add to Cart" or "$150 - Add to Cart." Remove the middle option (Handheld Showerhead + Filter Subscription at $135) from the visible buy box. If the product team needs to retain it, move it behind a "See all purchase options" text link below the ATC button. Directly below the ATC button, replace the current small grey trust bar with a visually distinct horizontal row of three trust items at a minimum of 14px font: "60-Day Risk-Free Returns | Free US Shipping | 1-Year Warranty." The HSA/FSA eligible line and the shipping date estimate remain below this trust row, unchanged. On mobile, the two toggle tabs ("Subscribe & Save" and "One-Time") are displayed side by side at full container width, each occupying 50%. The price and description line below the tabs update based on selection. The ATC button is full-width below the description. On desktop, the same two-tab layout applies within the existing buy box column, with the same spacing and stacking order.

## Slot 2: Homepage Hero: Product-First vs. Promo-First

**Type:** A/B test (1 variation vs. control)
**Page:** Homepage (getcanopy.co)
**Revenue potential:** Est. 60,000 sessions/month (organic, direct, remarketing) x 0.25% CR lift x $132 blended AOV = +$19,800/month.

**Hypothesis:** If we replace the current dark promo hero with a product-focused hero that leads with the Filtered Showerhead, a hard water dream outcome headline, and a single CTA, homepage conversion rate will increase because the current hero leads with a promotional mechanic, shows no product clearly, and presents four competing CTAs to new visitors.

**Data:** The current homepage hero is a dark full-width image of a showerhead with a dark overlay. The dominant text is "Buy one water filter, get one $25 off," a promotional mechanic rather than a product benefit. Four competing CTAs appear above the fold: "Shop Sale" (hero button), "Shop Now" (announcement bar), "Shop Now" (sticky bottom bar), and "Up to $25 Off" (popup) (Source: Site Screenshots). Amazon reviews identify hard water relief as the number one purchase driver, but "hard water" does not appear anywhere in the above-fold homepage copy (Source: Reviews). Google Search ads claim "Over 7,000 5-Star Reviews" but this number appears nowhere on the homepage above the fold (Source: Google Ads). The CRO ebook: "Make the product the Hero of the Hero Image. 100 percent of users will see your Hero Image and header." A dark overlay image without a clearly featured product fails this principle (Source: CRO Ebook).

**V1:** On desktop, replace the full-width dark hero with a two-column layout within the existing hero section height. The left column (50-55% width) contains a large product image of the Handheld Filtered Showerhead (chrome or black variant) on a light cream or off-white background with no dark overlay. The product is the central visual element, cropped to show the full showerhead head and handle, angled slightly. A small "Good Housekeeping Winner 2025" badge sits in the bottom-left corner of the product image. The right column (45-50% width) has a matching light cream background with the following content stacked vertically: a small uppercase eyebrow line at 12-13px reading "OVER 7,000 5-STAR REVIEWS"; a large serif headline reading "Hard water is destroying your hair. This is the fix."; a one-sentence subheadline reading "Canopy's filtered showerhead removes chlorine and minerals. Results after your first wash."; a single full-width black CTA button reading "Shop the Filtered Showerhead"; and below the button in small text: "From $125 with subscription. $150 one-time. Free US shipping." Remove the sticky bottom bar from the homepage fold. If the sticky bar is retained for scroll behavior, set it to appear only after the user has scrolled past the hero section. Remove the "Up to $25 Off" popup from the homepage hero view entirely. The announcement bar at the top remains unchanged (the sale is valid and should remain visible). On mobile, the layout stacks vertically: the product image occupies the top 40% of the screen height with a light background, then below it the eyebrow line, headline (same text), a shortened subheadline reading "Removes chlorine and minerals. Results after your first wash.", and a full-width black CTA button. No dark overlay. No popup. The sticky bottom bar, if retained, follows the same scroll-triggered behavior as desktop.

## Slot 3: Cart Subscription Upsell for One-Time Buyers

**Type:** A/B test (1 variation vs. control)
**Page:** Cart drawer (getcanopy.co, cart)
**Revenue potential:** Est. 5,000 cart sessions/month x 35% one-time buyers x 15% switch rate = 262 additional subscribers/month x $40/quarter x 4 quarters = +$41,920/year in new recurring subscription revenue. Does not include compounding LTV from retained subscribers.

**Hypothesis:** If we add a subscription upsell prompt for one-time buyers in the cart drawer and fix the cross-sell logic to exclude products already in the cart, subscription take rate will increase because there is currently no conversion path to subscription after the PDP for customers who chose one-time.

**Data:** The cart shows the Filtered Showerhead at $150 one-time with no subscription prompt anywhere in the cart (Source: Site Screenshots). The cross-sell section "Complete your home" recommends the Handheld Filtered Showerhead at $150, which is the same product already in the cart (Source: Site Screenshots). Switching from one-time to subscribe saves the customer $25 immediately ($150 to $125) and delivers filters and an aroma kit every 90 days at $40 ($77 value). This saving is not surfaced anywhere in the cart for one-time buyers (Source: Meta Ads, Site Screenshots). Research shows changing the default purchase option to subscribe lifted reorders +19% (Source: CRO Community Research). The cart also shows "Continue without free returns" as a link below the checkout button, which conflicts with the "Free returns within 60 days the U.S." note directly below it. This creates ambiguity about whether Canopy's standard return policy applies without the $5.19 package protection add-on (Source: Site Screenshots).

**V1:** Add a subscription upsell module directly above the Subtotal line in the cart drawer. The module has a teal left border matching Canopy's brand accent color and a very light teal background fill. It contains: a small calendar or subscription icon on the left; the text "Save $25: Switch to Subscribe & Save, $125 per month. Filters + Aroma Kit delivered every 90 days."; a full-width "Switch to Subscription" button in teal fill with white text; and one line below the button in small grey text reading "Cancel anytime." This module renders only when the cart contains a subscription-eligible product at the one-time price. When the user clicks "Switch to Subscription," the cart item updates to the subscription price and the module is replaced with a green confirmation line: "Switched to Subscribe & Save. $25 saved." Fix the "Complete your home" cross-sell section to exclude any product already in the cart. If the only recommended products are the same as what is in the cart, surface complementary products instead (for example, Baby Bath Tub Filter for a cart containing only the showerhead). Replace the "Continue without free returns" link text with "No thanks, skip package protection" to remove the false implication that Canopy's standard 60-day return policy is contingent on the $5.19 add-on. On mobile, the subscription upsell module is full-width and placed directly above the Subtotal row. On desktop, the same placement and width apply within the cart drawer panel.

## Future Slot Candidates

**Showerhead PDP: Hard water headline and speed-of-results stat above the fold.** The PDP headline is "Handheld Filtered Showerhead." A test replacing the headline or adding a hard water subheadline with the "results after your first wash" claim directly in the buy box area would directly address the number one purchase driver from reviews. This is a separate test from the buy box structure (Slot 1) and would isolate the messaging angle.

**Baby Bath Tub Filter PDP: Buy box simplification.** The Baby Bath Tub Filter PDP has the same nested buy box structure as the showerhead PDP. Two of the three Meta ads analyzed (running July 2025 and February 2026) land on this product. Applying the same simplification from Slot 1 to this PDP is a logical follow-on.

**Baby Bath Tub Filter Babylist traffic: Reroute to Canopy's own PDP.** Meta Ad 3 (running since February 26, 2026) sends paid traffic to Babylist.com, a third-party retailer with no subscription option, no first-party data capture, and no price control. Rerouting this ad to Canopy's own Baby Bath Tub Filter PDP would recover subscription revenue and first-party customer data from a campaign Canopy is already paying for.

**PDP page speed remediation (dev slot).** The showerhead PDP scores 11/100 on mobile. This is not an A/B test in the traditional sense. It is a technical remediation (image compression, JS deferral, render-blocking request reduction, lazy loading) that would benefit all paid traffic. Given that every 1-second delay reduces mobile CR by approximately 7%, speed remediation is likely the highest-leverage single action on the site and should be treated as a priority alongside these tests, not after them.

**Showerhead PDP: Social proof above the fold.** Google Search ads lead with "Over 7,000 5-Star Reviews" as a primary headline claim, signalling this is a proven trust hook for new visitors. That number does not appear on the PDP above the fold. A test adding an eyebrow line or review count badge (e.g., "7,000+ five-star reviews" with star glyphs) directly above the product title, or a compact social proof strip between the product title and the buy box, would carry the same trust signal from the ad into the landing page. This is a distinct test from the hard water headline candidate: it isolates social proof placement, not benefit messaging.
