# Veracity Health CRO Research Brief

**Data Sources:** Meta Ads (3 ads), Google Ads Transparency, Reviews & UGC, Page Speed/Core Web Vitals, Site Screenshots

## Insights

The data tells one clear story: Veracity has a high-conviction product with 2,000+ verified reviews, 8 independent clinical studies, and a GLP-1 alternative angle that both Meta and Google are spending against. What it doesn't have is a homepage that leads with why people actually buy, a PDP buy box that defaults to its best offer, or a cart that lets buyers check out without navigating around a subscription upsell first.

The biggest single insight is how uniform the review language is. Customers do not say "I bought this because of the 61% GLP-1 increase." They say "food noise is gone," "easy transition off my shots," and "clean energy with no jitters." This language appears across dozens of reviews and across three separate buyer segments: GLP-1 transitioners, postpartum moms, and perimenopausal women. None of it appears above the fold on the homepage or in the buy box on the PDP. The homepage hero reads "1200+ 5-STAR REVIEWS / On Bestselling Metabolism Ignite." That is social proof, not a dream outcome — and the count is stale. The PDP shows 2,000+ reviews.

Two more findings converge on the PDP buy box. First: the default pre-selected tier is the 1-month subscription at $63.75/mo — the most expensive per-bottle option and the weakest offer on the page. The 6-month "Doctor Recommended" tier at $37.50/mo (50% off) requires an active selection to access. Multiple reviews cite price as a concern. The brand's own Jurni landing page (used for Ad 3) already de-emphasizes the 1-month by showing 3/6-month options first — but the main PDP, where most paid traffic lands, still defaults to 1-month. Second: the PDP's mobile lab score is 27/100, with an LCP of 16.9s and a 10MB network payload. Most paid social traffic arrives on mobile. A page that takes 17 seconds to load loses a meaningful share of visitors before the buy box even renders.

In the cart, the primary action is "SUBSCRIBE & SAVE 30% ($117 OFF OVER 3 MONTHS)" — a large orange button positioned above the Checkout button. One-time buyers must look past a subscription commitment ask before they can complete their purchase. The upsell banner at the top of the cart promotes the Metabolic Power Stack to a shopper who already has both products in the cart.

Conservative math: if the PDP sees 10,000 sessions per month at a 2.5% baseline CR and a $75 AOV, a 0.5% CR lift generates $3,750/month. A shift of 20% of single-month subscribers to the 6-month tier adds roughly $7,500/month in AOV. These are estimates pending access to real session data, but the direction is clear. The three tests below go after the biggest drops in the funnel.

## Slot 1: Metabolism Ignite PDP — Customer Verbatim in Buy Zone

**Type:** A/B test (1 variation vs. control)
**Page:** Metabolism Ignite PDP (veracityhealth.co/pages/metabolism-ignite-12), Jurni LP variant (veracityhealth.co/apps/jurni/pdp)
**Revenue potential:** Estimated 10,000 PDP sessions/month x 2.5% baseline CR x $75 AOV = $18,750/month baseline. A 0.5% CR lift = $3,750/month. A 1% lift (consistent with review placement tests averaging +14% relative CR lift per Brillmark, 2025) = $7,500/month. [Session volume is an estimate; verify against actual analytics.]

**Hypothesis:** If we place a single featured customer quote — in buyer language, not clinical language — directly above the subscription tier selector, more visitors will choose a subscription tier and add to bag, because the quote mirrors the exact language the highest-intent buyer segments use to describe their purchase motivation.

**Data:** Reviews show that "food noise," "easy transition off GLP-1 shots," and "clean energy without jitters" are the top three reasons customers buy — yet the buy box above-fold copy reads "Clinically backed, natural GLP-1 support for appetite control and weight loss. Made from safe, non-drug ingredients." with checkmarks for "Burns fat," "Curbs cravings," "Side effect free." These are functional descriptions, not outcomes. The most helpful review on Amazon (82 helpful votes, July 2025) reads: "No jitters, no aftertaste. Decreased appetite for junk. Meals keep me fuller for longer. 10/10." (Source: Reviews) A separate review from January 2026 (12 helpful votes) reads: "I was nervous about my food noise coming back after stopping GLP-1 shots. Within a week it was such an easy transition." (Source: Reviews) Community research shows review placement is the single highest-impact PDP test category, averaging +14% relative CR lift when reviews are moved to more prominent positions (Brillmark, 2025). The ebook notes that users typically read 5-10 reviews before deciding, and that social proof placed near the buy box closes buyers faster than social proof placed lower on the page. (Source: CRO Ebook)

**V1:** Add a single featured review quote between the product checkmarks ("Burns fat / Curbs cravings / Side effect free") and the "Choose Quantity" tier selector. The quote reads: "I was nervous about my food noise coming back after stopping GLP-1 shots. Within a week it was such an easy transition." — Hannah N., Verified Buyer, January 2026 (★★★★★). The quote is presented as a simple text block: orange quote marks, customer name in small type, star rating inline. No new section is added. Nothing else in the buy box changes — tiers, CTAs, gift display, and cross-sell remain identical to control.

On desktop: the quote block sits in a single-line or two-line text band between the checkmarks row and the "Choose Quantity" label. It is left-aligned and sized to match the surrounding buy box text (approximately 14-16px). The rest of the right column is unchanged.

On mobile: same placement. The quote appears as a short text block between the checkmarks and the quantity selector, visible without scrolling for most device sizes. The sticky bottom bar ("Select a Quantity / Add To Bag") is unchanged.

## Slot 2: Homepage — Dream Outcome Hero Headline

**Type:** A/B test (1 variation vs. control)
**Page:** Homepage (veracityhealth.co/)
**Revenue potential:** Estimated 20,000 homepage sessions/month. A 10% improvement in PDP CTR from the hero = 2,000 additional PDP sessions. At 2.5% CR and $75 AOV = $3,750/month. [Session volume is an estimate; verify against actual analytics.]

**Hypothesis:** If we replace the hero headline with a dream outcome statement drawn directly from the top customer review themes and update the stale review count to the current figure, more visitors will click through to the PDP, because the headline matches the language of the buyer rather than describing an asset the brand has.

**Data:** The current hero headline reads "★★★★★ / 1200+ 5-STAR REVIEWS / On Bestselling Metabolism Ignite." Per the ebook: "Headlines are supposed to tell your customers the end result of using your product." Social proof in a headline is a secondary signal; it should follow the dream outcome, not replace it. (Source: CRO Ebook) Reviews clearly state the dream outcome: "food noise is gone," "natural alternative to injections without side effects," "clean energy." (Source: Reviews) Google is actively running the headline "Feel Full Longer Naturally" on Search — an outcome-based message — but the homepage hero doesn't mirror this language. (Source: Google Ads Transparency) The review count in the hero (1,200+) is also stale. The PDP shows 2,000+ verified reviews, and the Jurni LP variant shows 2,006. Understating the review count understates trust at the highest-traffic entry point on the site. (Source: Site Screenshots)

**V1:** Replace the hero headline and CTA button label. All other elements in the hero remain unchanged: product imagery, lime green offer badge ("Save up to 50%, plus $75 in Free Gifts"), orange background, announcement bar.

New headline (replacing "★★★★★ / 1200+ 5-STAR REVIEWS / On Bestselling Metabolism Ignite"): two lines — "Silence Food Noise. Lose Weight Without the Shots." — in the same font size and position as the current headline. Below the headline, the star rating and review count line updates to: "★★★★★ 2,000+ Verified Reviews" (replacing "1200+" with the current figure). The CTA button label changes from "Shop Now" to "Get Metabolism Ignite."

On desktop: the new headline occupies the lower-left of the hero in the same position and approximate character count. The review count update is a single line below the headline. CTA button text change only — size, color, and position are unchanged.

On mobile: same copy changes. On mobile, the hero is likely stacked vertically; the headline is the most prominent text element. The CTA button is full-width below the headline and review count line. The offer badge remains in its position on the product imagery side of the hero.

## Slot 3: Cart Drawer — Checkout as Primary CTA

**Type:** A/B test (1 variation vs. control)
**Page:** Cart Drawer (site-wide, all pages)
**Revenue potential:** Estimated 2,000 cart sessions/month. A 3% improvement in checkout rate = 60 additional checkouts. At $130 AOV (as observed in cart screenshot) = $7,800/month. [Cart session volume is an estimate; verify against actual analytics.]

**Hypothesis:** If we make the Checkout button the primary action in the cart and reposition the subscription offer as a secondary option, checkout completion rate will increase, because one-time buyers will no longer need to navigate past a subscription commitment ask before completing their purchase.

**Data:** The current cart drawer shows "SUBSCRIBE & SAVE 30% ($117 OFF OVER 3 MONTHS)" as a large orange button — the same visual weight and color as the "CHECKOUT" button below it — positioned above the checkout action. This sequence asks visitors to evaluate a subscription offer at the moment of highest purchase intent. Community research flags this pattern as a cart abandonment driver: "upsell apps can result in higher rates of abandoned carts and lower conversion rates" (Multiple sources, 2026). The ebook states: "If an upsell element has a lower Revenue Per Session than the Add to Cart Button, the upsell is hurting total revenue." (Source: CRO Ebook) A separate issue reinforces the problem: the current cart also shows a banner promoting the "Metabolic Power Stack" to a shopper who already has the Metabolism + Power Protein Set (which contains both products) in their cart. This irrelevant upsell is noise at the checkout stage. (Source: Site Screenshots) Additionally, there is a large empty white space between the cart item and the subtotal with no review, cross-sell, or urgency element.

**V1:** Swap the CTA hierarchy and add a brief review snippet in the empty space.

The "CHECKOUT" button becomes the primary full-width orange button at the bottom of the cart (in the position currently occupied by the subscribe button). The subscription offer moves below the product list and above the subtotal, presented as a secondary styled card — lighter background (gray or off-white), smaller text, outline-style button: "Save $117 over 3 months — switch to subscription" with a "Subscribe Instead" link-style CTA. The irrelevant upsell banner ("Get Up to 40% Off the Metabolic Power Stack") is removed. In the empty white space above the subtotal, a single short review snippet is added: "No more food noise. Meals keep me fuller for longer. 10/10." — Chloe, Verified Buyer ★★★★★.

On desktop: the cart right-panel shows the product item at top, then the review snippet, then the subscribe secondary card, then subtotal + free shipping line, then the primary "CHECKOUT" orange button full-width at the bottom.

On mobile: the same layout stacks vertically. The subscribe offer card is visible on scroll but clearly secondary. The CHECKOUT button is the primary bottom-fixed or near-bottom action. The review snippet sits above the subtotal in the middle of the cart.

## Future Slot Candidates

These are data-supported ideas that didn't make the top 3. Queue for next roadmap discussion.

1. **PDP — Pre-select 6-Month Subscription Tier as Default.** The PDP pre-selects 1-month ($63.75/mo, 15% off). The Doctor Recommended 6-month tier ($37.50/mo, 50% off) requires active selection. Testing 6-month as the default could increase AOV significantly. Risk: may reduce conversion rate for buyers who want to trial before committing. Best tested after Slot 1 to isolate impact.

2. **PDP — Elevate FAQ Section.** All product information is buried in collapsed accordion sections (Key Ingredients, Who It's For, Benefits, How to Use, FAQs). The ebook cites FAQ repositioning higher on landing pages as a frequent large revenue lift. Key objections from reviews — does it work for nursing moms? how long before results? — are likely answered in the FAQ but invisible until clicked. Test: expand and move the top 3-4 FAQ items above the trust bar.

3. **Message Match LP Variant — Ad-Specific Discount Callout.** Two of the three Meta ads analyzed have discount % mismatches with their landing pages (Ad 1 promises 35% off; Ad 2 promises 20% off). An LP variant that surfaces the specific discount mentioned in the source ad could close this trust gap at first touch. Requires UTM-based LP routing.

4. **PDP Mobile Speed Remediation.** PDP lab performance is 27/100 with LCP 16.9s and a 10MB network payload on mobile. JavaScript execution time is 8.6s. Primary culprits: render-blocking requests (2,740ms savings potential), 1,296 KiB of unused JavaScript, and unoptimized images (934 KiB savings). This is a development task, not an A/B test — but it is the highest-leverage investment in the entire funnel given all paid traffic lands on this page on mobile. Recommend prioritizing alongside CRO testing.

5. **Collection Page — Review Counts and Max Discount on Cards.** Collection cards show "Subscribe and save 15%" (the minimum rate) and lack visible star ratings in the scrolled state. Updating cards to show "Save up to 50%" and surface review counts could improve CTR to PDP from organic/browse traffic.
