CRO Testing Roadmap

June 2026

Three high-volume touchpoints are leaking revenue before purchase intent converts: the PDP buy box defaults to the lowest-value option, the compression landing page loads in 8.4 seconds on mobile, and the cart's AOV mechanic prompts visitors with no product to act on. Eight test slots address these gaps in evidence-strength order.

Slot 1 A/B Test

PDP Buy Box - Pre-Select Best Value Tier

Compression Socks PDP

The buy box stacks three pricing tiers between the size selector and Add to Cart, with the lowest-value 1-pair option pre-selected by default. The 4-pair "BEST VALUE" tier carries a text label but no visual distinction, requiring a three-way pricing decision before a visitor commits.

Cross-Source Analysis

Biggest Killers of Conversion Rate

  • 1 Paid traffic landing on an 8.4-second page. Lighthouse scores the compression LP at 8.4s LCP on mobile, 2.8x Google's 3-second abandonment threshold. All Meta compression ads and Google edema queries route to this page first.
  • 2 Buy box defaults to the lowest-value option. The 1-pair tier at $49.99 is pre-selected. Visitors must make a three-way pricing decision before they have decided to buy at all.
  • 3 Cart AOV mechanic with no payoff. The progress bar prompts visitors to add one more pair to unlock two free pairs, but roughly 40% of the cart drawer height is blank white space with no product to act on.
  • 4 Sizing cuts out the primary medical audience. Calf fit failure is the single most common negative review theme. Edema, post-surgery, and DVT buyers statistically have larger legs, and the PDP gives them no inline fit reassurance.
  • 5 6,333 reviews are invisible at the moment of decision. The buy box shows a linked count. No snippet, no star breakdown, and no featured quote appear before the Add to Cart button.

Findings by Source

Meta Ads

  • Three ads are active across Facebook, Instagram, Messenger, and Reels. Ad 1 (compression angle, running since June 1) uses a problem-agitate frame on tightness, overheating, and sliding. It routes to the compression LP with strong message match: the ad's three pain points mirror the comparison table's first three rows.
  • The longest-running ad (Father's Day gift frame, active since May 29) has no captured landing page. Message match for this ad cannot be evaluated.
  • Ad 3's countdown timer reads "THIS WEEKEND" on a 4th of July-framed creative. For retargeted visitors who see this ad after the holiday, the countdown is a broken urgency signal.

Google Ads

  • Three ad formats are active: video, search, and shopping. Keyword targeting is broader than Meta, with condition-specific queries (edema, hyperhidrosis) and audience-specific terms (construction workers, ski socks) alongside comfort-led video ads.
  • The Shopping ad shows 4.0 stars from 28 reviews, vs. 6,383 reviews on-site. A Merchant Center feed fix could improve Shopping CTR at no creative cost.
  • Google leads with comfort and sensation ("no squeeze," "no itching"); Meta leads with clinical comparison and material science. These are complementary angles serving different buyer awareness levels.

Reviews and UGC

  • 80 Amazon reviews collected. The distribution is bimodal: 37 five-star reviews and 27 one-or-two-star reviews, with a smaller cluster in the middle. Primary praise centers on comfort, ease of donning, and medical outcomes. "After having quadruple heart surgery, my swelling has totally gone away." "These are the first compression socks I can actually put on easily."
  • Calf fit failure is the most common negative theme. Over 10 of 27 negative reviews cite inability to pull the sock past the calf from buyers with edema, DVT, or post-surgery conditions. The primary medical use case correlates with larger legs, making this the deepest product-audience mismatch in the review pool.
  • Secondary complaints: compression inadequacy in some buyer experiences ("no compression, except a little bit of grabbiness at the very top band"), durability issues after first wash, and price-value friction from buyers who paid full price and later encountered the BOGO promotion.

Page Speed Insights

  • Homepage: Performance 39/100. FCP 3.7s, LCP 5.9s, TTI 33.4s. Compression LP: Performance 42/100. FCP 3.9s, LCP 8.4s, TTI 33.2s. Both pages score in the bottom quartile for ecommerce performance.
  • The compression LP's 8.4s LCP is the most critical number in the audit. This page receives all paid Meta and Google compression traffic, and at this load time a significant share of paid sessions abandon before the comparison table or offer renders.
  • One positive: CLS is 0 on both pages. PDP performance was not tested, leaving a gap for the primary conversion page from organic and retargeting traffic.

Site Screenshots

  • Homepage trust signals (21,000+ 5-star reviews, 3 million+ pairs sold) sit below the fold. Visitors who bounce early on the hero never encounter them.
  • Collection page: every product card carries the "BUY 2 GET 2 FREE" badge, reducing urgency through repetition. Star ratings display inconsistently across visible product cards.
  • PDP: 6,333 reviews appear as a linked count only. The three-tier pricing block sits between size selection and Add to Cart, creating a sequential decision sequence before commitment. The "BEST VALUE" 4-pair tier is labeled but not pre-selected.
  • Cart drawer: the progress bar mechanic is present and correct. The cart body below the single item is roughly 40% blank white space with no product recommendations or cross-sell tiles rendering.

Competitor Analysis

  • The nearest price competitor (Clohill) offers compression socks at approximately $7 per pair in multi-packs vs. Hollow's $25 per pair at the 4-pack price point. The pricing gap is significant for buyers who discover it mid-consideration.
  • Hollow's primary moat: USA-made positioning, 21,000+ reviews, and a clinical compression angle. If compression claims are challenged in reviews, the premium price point loses its primary defense against lower-cost alternatives.
