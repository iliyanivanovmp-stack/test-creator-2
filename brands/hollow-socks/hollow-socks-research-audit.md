## Source Findings

### Meta Ads & Landing Pages

Three ads active as of early June 2026. All run on Facebook, Instagram, Messenger, and Reels/Threads.

**Ad 1 (compression angle, since Jun 1):** Problem-agitate frame. Opens with "Compression socks aren't supposed to hurt" and pivots to sock-specific pain — tightness, overheating, sliding. Visual: side-by-side legs showing swelling before/after. CTA: Shop Now. Landing page is the primary compression LP (hollowsocks.com/pages/10r-compression-g1), which leads with "10 Reasons Why America Is Ditching Their Doctor's Synthetic Compression Socks For Alpaca." Comparison table in Fold 2 directly mirrors ad pain points (tight bands, red marks, uniform vs. graduated compression). Message match is strong — the ad's three pain points are the first three rows of the comparison table. Father's Day promo consistent across ad and LP announcement bar.

**Ad 2 (Father's Day gift frame, since May 29):** Sketch-style illustration creative. Leads with gift framing and "Buy 2 Get 2 Free." Sub-headline targets odor: "Finally. A gift that fixes his smelly feet problem for good." Landing page was not captured — message match cannot be evaluated. Gap noted.

**Ad 3 (patriotic/seasonal, since Jun 1):** Patriotic poster visual, Uncle Sam parody referencing alpaca fiber. Copy leads with material superiority: moisture-wicking, odor resistance, softness. Landing page opens with "10 Reasons Why American-Made Alpaca Socks Dominate Merino & Cotton." Material benefits (hollow fiber, heat release at 75°F, 3x faster drying vs. cotton) run through the LP's reason list. Strong message match on material angle. One inconsistency: ad says "4th of July Sale" but LP countdown reads "THIS WEEKEND" — if the countdown timer runs on a fixed date it will show an expired or mismatched deadline to post-4th visitors, eroding urgency credibility.

**LP structure overall:** Both captured LPs use a long-form editorial format ("10 Reasons") with an author byline and comparison table above the fold. The format converts cold traffic by leading with a credibility anchor (doctor/synthetic comparison) before presenting the product. The Buy 2 Get 2 Free offer is persistent throughout.

### Google Ads

Three screenshots show a mix of video/display and search/shopping formats. Advertiser: Hollow Alpaca LLC.

**Headline themes:** "Compression Support WITHOUT the Squeeze. Soft, breathable, and easy to slip on" dominates video ads. Seasonal variant: "The Best Gift for Dads." Search headline: "Hollow Alpaca Compression Sock — 14+ Hours. No Swelling." Shopping ad shows $49.99, 4.0 stars (28+ reviews), with shipping date.

**Keyword targeting:** Broader than Meta. Condition-specific queries active: hyperhidrosis, edema/leg swelling. Audience-specific: construction work, ski socks. This signals intent-based demand capture beyond the warm social audience Meta targets.

**Creative approach:** Heavy UGC-style video — creators holding socks, talking to camera with overlaid callouts ("NO ITCHING NO OVERHEATING ZERO STINK," "NO MORE SWAMP FOOT"). Social proof testimonials embedded in video: "Thanks to whoever said alpaca compression socks wouldn't hurt my legs... this is compression done right!"

**Gaps vs. Meta:** Google leans on comfort/sensation ("no squeeze," "no itching"). Meta leads with clinical comparison and material science. These are complementary angles, not redundant — Google catches demand-aware buyers, Meta educates. However, the shopping ad rating (4.0 stars, 28 reviews) diverges significantly from the 6,383 reviews and implied 4.7+ star rating surfaced on the PDP — likely the Google Merchant Center product listing hasn't been fully optimized to reflect the full review corpus.

### Reviews & UGC

Source: Amazon, 80 reviews, collected 2026-06-02.
Distribution: 5★ 37 | 4★ 4 | 3★ 12 | 2★ 10 | 1★ 17 (bimodal — 51% 5-star, 34% 1-2 star).

#### What Customers Love

- **Comfort and softness:** Multiple 5-star reviewers call these the most comfortable compression socks they've owned. "Most other compression socks have been skin tight and very uncomfortable. These are just the opposite." — Edward Balen. "I tried one pair, and now own 7 — they've become my almost-everyday socks of choice." — Joe B.
- **Ease of donning:** Repeatedly cited as a differentiator vs. typical medical compression socks. "These are the first compression socks I can actually put on easily." — Joe B. "Easy to put on. Give good support." — Larry D.J.
- **Swelling and circulation results:** High-medical-need buyers report strong outcomes. "After having quadruple heart surgery... these socks my swelling has totally gone away!!" — Joe Stinger. "After 3 days of wear, swelling has decreased, and legs feel a lot better." — Ronald Woodward.
- **USA-made:** Mentioned unprompted in multiple positive reviews. "This is one of the few products I have purchased from Amazon that is actually made in the USA." — Jack Nicholson.
- **Temperature regulation:** Consistent praise for all-season comfort. "True to size and keep my feet warm in the winter and cool in the summer. A real all-season solution." — Nlsimo.

#### What Frustrates Customers

- **Sizing too small for large calves (most common complaint):** Multiple 1-2 star reviews cite inability to pull the sock past the calf. "Do not fit wide calves. Largest size supposed to fit, cannot get them up onto my calves." — Anthony P. "Well made, but unusable for large people... I purchased the largest pair I could, the Extra Large, and they do not fit over my calf." — Brian. "Not for larger calves. If you have any circulation problem, your legs are probably going to be bigger and these are not for you!" — Amazon Customer. This is the deepest irony in the product: the primary medical use case (edema, DVT, post-surgery) correlates with larger legs, but the sizing cuts out a significant share of that target buyer.
- **Compression inadequacy vs. claims:** Several reviews say the socks feel like regular socks. "There is no compression, except a little bit of grabbiness at the very top band." — P. Peters. "They fall to ankle from the calf muscle after an hour of wearing them." — Gary J. Glomski. "Doesn't live up to advertisement." — Joe.
- **Durability after washing:** Two distinct issues: (1) losing elasticity after first wash, and (2) pilling. "After washing... I was hearing 'popping' as I was pulling them on as if elastic were breaking. Not worth the $300 for the 13 pair I bought." — RichL. "They are starting to pill up. It seems to be happening from wear, not from washing." — P. Peters.
- **Price-to-value mismatch:** "Compared to other compression socks I wear, I don't see spending $40 more a pair." — Robert Winstead. "Very expensive and not very durable." — Randall L. Notably, one reviewer flagged buying at full price then seeing the Buy 2 Get 2 Free promo appear: "After I paid full price $49.95, they are now marketing Buy 2 Get 2 Free." — L55MEDIA.

#### Client-Actionable Insights

- **Sizing chart is misleading for edema/medical buyers:** The primary medical audience (post-surgery, DVT, chronic edema) has larger legs. The XL cap likely excludes a disproportionate share of high-need, high-value customers. Product: consider adding XXL or a dedicated wide-calf SKU. Copy: add explicit calf circumference ranges to the sizing selector on PDP.
- **Durability after washing degrades retention:** Multiple repeat buyers lost trust after first wash. Product/ops: review yarn twist and elastic band sourcing. Support: proactively send care instruction reminder post-purchase before first wash cycle.
- **Compression perception gap:** Some buyers receive enough compression, others don't. The clinical 15-20 mmHg claim isn't felt the same way by all body types. Consider including a "how to know if you're getting compression" card in fulfillment.
- **Amazon pricing friction (full price vs. promo timing):** Buyers who paid $49.99 then saw the Buy 2 Get 2 Free promo felt price-burned. Consider a loyalty discount or early-access offer to direct-site repeat buyers.

### PageSpeed / Core Web Vitals

Source: Lighthouse audit, collected with cro-collect.

**Homepage — hollowsocks.com:**
- Performance: 39/100
- FCP: 3.7s | LCP: 5.9s | TBT: 1,120ms | CLS: 0 | Speed Index: 10.2s | TTI: 33.4s

**Primary Compression Landing Page — hollowsocks.com/pages/10r-compression-g1:**
- Performance: 42/100
- FCP: 3.9s | LCP: 8.4s | TBT: 730ms | CLS: 0 | Speed Index: 8.8s | TTI: 33.2s

Both pages score in the bottom quartile (sub-50). The landing page LCP of 8.4s is critical — this is the primary destination for paid Meta and Google traffic. Industry benchmark for ecommerce LCP is under 2.5s. At 8.4s, a meaningful portion of paid visitors will bounce before the page is usable. Google's data shows 53% of mobile users abandon pages that take longer than 3s to load. TBT of 1,120ms on the homepage signals heavy JavaScript execution blocking interactivity.

The only positive: CLS is 0 on both tested pages, meaning no visible layout shifts on load.

PDP performance was not tested separately — a gap given it is the primary conversion page from organic and retargeting traffic.

### Current Site Screenshots

**Homepage:**
Hero is a lifestyle image (father and son outdoors) with strong emotional resonance for the Father's Day campaign. The announcement bar countdown and "Buy 2 Get 2 Free" CTA are prominent above the fold. However, the only above-the-fold CTA is the promo button — there is no standalone "Shop Compression" or category entry point. Benefit icons and the social proof bar (21,000+ 5-star reviews, 3M+ pairs sold) sit below the fold, meaning first-time visitors who bounce early never encounter the strongest trust signals.

**Collection Page:**
Category navigation strip (Best Sellers, Compression, Everyday, Trades, Bundles, Running, Hunting) provides clear wayfinding. The product grid is functional but visually noisy — every card carries the "BUY 2 GET 2 FREE" badge, which reduces its urgency signal through repetition. Star ratings are shown inconsistently: one card in the visible grid shows the review count (1,305), others show none. No filter or sort controls are visible above the grid, which matters for buyers navigating a 10+ SKU catalog by use case or size.

**PDP (Compression Socks):**
6,333 reviews are cited at the top of the buy box but no review snippet or star breakdown is shown — only the linked count. The pricing tier block sits between the size selector and the Add to Cart button, creating a three-decision sequence (size → width → pricing tier) before a buyer can add to cart. The "best value" 4-pair tier is labeled as such but is not pre-selected. The benefit icons (Reduce Swelling, Built to Move, Support Circulation, Less Downtime) appear below the ATC button — they are visible on scroll but not at the moment of purchase decision. A sticky footer ATC is present, which partially mitigates the friction.

**Cart (drawer):**
The AOV mechanic is present: a progress bar reads "ADD 1 PAIR MORE TO UNLOCK 2 FREE PAIRS" with milestone icons. This is directionally correct — it creates upsell urgency at checkout. However, the cart body shows a large blank white area below the single item. No product recommendations or cross-sell tiles are rendered. Based on the visual gap, this is either an unimplemented cross-sell widget or a failed load. If the latter, this is a live revenue leak: every single-item cart that reaches checkout without being prompted to add a second pair is a missed AOV opportunity.

## Cross-Source Themes

Ranked by evidence strength × revenue potential × funnel importance:

**1. Sizing credibility gap at point of purchase (PDP + Reviews)**
The most common 1-2 star theme is inability to fit the sock over large calves. The irony: the primary medical audience (edema, post-surgery, DVT patients) statistically has larger legs. The LP addresses this in Reason 5 ("Wide Calf & Women's Sizes Included") but the PDP buy box offers no equivalent reassurance. Buyers who don't scroll the LP never see it. Evidence: 10+ Amazon reviews, LP copy, PDP layout.

**2. PDP pricing friction blocking the add-to-cart (PDP + Site)**
Three stacked pricing tiers sit between size selection and the ATC button. The tiers represent real value (1-pair at $49.99 vs. 4-pair at $25/pair) but require a buy-decision (bundle vs. single) before a visitor has decided to buy at all. The "BEST VALUE" label on the 4-pair tier is not pre-selected. Evidence: PDP visual summary, live site fetch.

**3. Cart cross-sell failure suppressing AOV (Cart + LPs)**
The progress bar mechanic correctly prompts adding a second pair to unlock free pairs, but no product recommendation renders in the cart body to act on that prompt. Every single-item cart gets the prompt but not the path. Evidence: cart visual summary, AOV mechanic analysis.

## Top Test Opportunities

Ranked by evidence strength × revenue potential × fixability.

**PDP Buy Box Restructure — Default to 4-Pair (Best Value) Tier** — The buy box forces visitors to actively choose between three pricing tiers before adding to cart. Pre-selecting the 4-pair "Best Value" bundle and repositioning 1-pair as a secondary option could lift AOV and reduce decision friction simultaneously. Evidence: PDP visual summary, pricing structure (live site). Est. lift: 8% CR improvement on add-to-cart × [sessions/mo unknown] × $100 AOV = [calculate at audit completion].

**PDP Size-Fit Reassurance Module** — Large-calf sizing is the #1 review complaint, yet the buy box shows only "Standard/Wide" labels with no calf circumference ranges or visual fit guide near the selector. Adding explicit calf circumference ranges (e.g., "Wide = up to 17" calf") and a one-line fit guarantee adjacent to the width selector directly addresses the hesitation that drives 1-2 star reviews and returns. Evidence: 10+ Amazon reviews, LP Reason 5, PDP visual summary. Est. lift: 5–10% CR improvement for wide-calf segment.

**Cart Cross-Sell Widget (implement or repair)** — The cart progress bar creates urgency to add a second pair but renders no product to act on it. Implementing (or fixing) a product recommendation widget showing the top 2 complementary SKUs (e.g., Crew Socks, Trades Socks) would give the progress bar mechanic a payoff. Evidence: cart visual summary (empty white space), progress bar mechanic. Est. lift: 10–15% AOV increase on single-item carts.

**Landing Page Speed Remediation (LCP 8.4s)** — The primary paid traffic landing page loads in 8.4s on mobile. Every paid click from Meta and Google reaches this page first. At this LCP, a significant share of paid sessions abandon before seeing the comparison table or offer. Reducing LCP to under 3s via image optimization and above-the-fold render prioritization would recover those lost sessions. Evidence: Lighthouse audit (pagespeed.md). Est. lift: estimated 10–20% paid conversion improvement based on LCP benchmarks.

**PDP Social Proof Anchor Near ATC** — 6,333 reviews are cited at the top of the buy box but only as a number. No star breakdown, no review snippet, and no "what customers say about compression" callout appears near the ATC button. Adding a 1–2 sentence pulled review (e.g., Joe Stinger's post-surgery swelling testimonial) directly above or below the ATC would reinforce purchase confidence at the moment of decision. Evidence: PDP visual summary, review pool.

## Unused but Valuable Findings

- Google Shopping ad shows 4.0 stars / 28 reviews — significantly lower than the 6,383-review corpus shown on-site. If corrected, this could improve Shopping CTR and search ad credibility at no conversion cost.
- Amazon reviews flag the price-burn issue (buyers who paid full price then saw Buy 2 Get 2 Free) — a loyalty or early-access email to past buyers could recover this segment before they churn to Clohill's lower price point.
- Ad 3 LP countdown reads "THIS WEEKEND" on a 4th of July-framed ad — if the countdown is static or misconfigured post-holiday, this creates a broken urgency signal for retargeted visitors.
