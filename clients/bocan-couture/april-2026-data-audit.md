## Google Ads (Google Ads Transparency Centre, March 2026)

**Overview:** 38 ads running across Google platforms. Managed by Mith Digital LTD (verified advertiser). Mix of Search ads, Shopping ads, and one local/maps ad for the London boutique. No YouTube ads, no Display ads, no Performance Max visible.

**Ad formats breakdown:**
- **Search ads (majority):** Generic text ads targeting broad terms like "luxury nightwear," "maternity dresses," "bridal wear," "home wear." Multiple sitelink extensions pointing to collection pages (Nightgowns, Bridal Collection, Pyjama Sets, Silk Collection).
- **Shopping ads:** Product listing ads showing individual products (e.g., "Georgia Navy Blue Silk Cami," "Vela Silky Robe Set Off White"). Prices show as [Price]/[Original currency] in Transparency Centre, so actual displayed prices are unclear.
- **Local ad:** One maps/location ad promoting "Bocan Couture - London" with Marylebone pin, store hours (10AM-6PM), and "Free International Shipping" callout.

**Critical issue: Google seller rating shows 3.6 stars (20 reviews).** Bocan has 4.5 stars on Trustpilot from 112 reviews, but the Google seller rating pulling into search ads is 3.6 from only 20 reviews. This is the rating potential customers see before they even click the ad. For a luxury brand at £1,689 AOV, 3.6 stars actively damages trust. The Trustpilot score is not feeding into Google's seller ratings. This needs immediate attention: either connect Trustpilot as a Google-approved review partner, collect Google-native reviews, or investigate which review source is generating the 3.6 rating.

**Ad copy problems:**

1. **Generic, repetitive copy across most ads.** Multiple search ads use nearly identical descriptions: "Shop online new collection! Nightgowns, Pajama Sets, Bridal Collections. Custom Products." and "Shop online new collection! Nightgowns, Pajama Sets, Bridal Collections. Free Shipping. Feel Pure Elegance with BOCAN!" This reads like fast fashion, not a brand selling £1,689 robes. No differentiation, no luxury positioning, no reason to click over competitors.

2. **No emotional or brand story copy.** The Meta ads at least reference "Designed in London," "handcrafted," and lifestyle moments. The Google search ads are purely transactional. One exception: "Each Piece Is Thoughtfully Designed To Enhance Your Wardrobe With Elegance That Lasts Celebrate Your Femininity in Beauty. Elegance Isn't Solely Defined by What You Wear." But even this reads as filler rather than specifics about materials, craftsmanship, or what makes Bocan different.

3. **Discount code in ads undermines luxury positioning.** One ad shows "10% off All Products · Code BOCAN10" as a promotion extension. For a luxury brand, leading with discounts in search ads erodes perceived value. Customers who find you through a "10% off" prompt are price-anchored from the start.

4. **No social proof in ad copy.** No mention of Trustpilot reviews, customer count, years in business, or any credibility signal. At this AOV, search ads need to pre-qualify the click with trust.

5. **"Luxury Duvet Sets" ad dilutes focus.** One ad promotes bedding ("Luxury Duvet Sets - Super King, Cotton BedSet"), which expands the product range but may confuse the brand story if the core positioning is luxury nightwear and homewear.

**What's working:**
- Sitelink extensions are well-structured, pointing to specific collections (Nightgowns, Bridal, Pyjama Sets, Silk Collection, The Bridal Edit, Maternity Edit).
- The London store local ad is smart for driving foot traffic and building local credibility.
- Category coverage is broad: maternity, bridal, nightgowns, home wear, silk. They're capturing intent across their product range.

**Missing opportunities:**
- **YouTube ads:** Zero YouTube presence visible. For a luxury brand with strong visual product and lifestyle angles, YouTube is a natural fit. Even repurposing existing Meta video content (like the London boutique video already running on Meta) as YouTube ads would expand reach at similar CPMs.
- **No remarketing/Display visible:** No display or remarketing ads appear in Transparency Centre. Given that 98.83% of visitors leave without adding to cart, remarketing with lifestyle imagery and social proof could recapture high-intent visitors.
- **No Performance Max campaigns visible:** PMax would allow Google to optimize across Search, Shopping, YouTube, Display, and Discover from a single campaign, which is particularly effective for luxury e-commerce with strong visual assets.

**Message match issue (same as Meta):** The ad copy makes generic promises ("Shop online new collection!") and lands visitors on pages that are equally generic. There is no continuity between ad intent and landing page experience. A visitor searching "luxury maternity robes" clicks an ad, lands on a collection page with 126 products and no maternity-specific context. The same trust gap identified in Meta ads exists here, compounded by weaker ad copy.

At 80% mobile traffic from the Gulf region, a 5.9s LCP is severe. Google recommends under 2.5s. The 13.7s Speed Index means the page takes nearly 14 seconds to visually render on mobile. This could partially explain the 8s homepage engagement time in GA4: visitors may be leaving before the page even finishes loading.

The mobile preview also reveals two overlays stacking on first load: a 10% off popup and a cookie consent banner. That is two interruptions before the visitor has seen any content.

### Render Blocking and Load Time Are Hurting Conversion Rate

760ms Total Blocking Time means the page is unresponsive for nearly a full second while scripts execute. 5.9s LCP means the main hero image takes almost 6 seconds to appear. For a luxury brand where first impressions are everything, this is damaging.

**Three areas to investigate:**

**1. Third-Party Script Load**

The homepage loads the following third-party scripts on page load:

- **Trustpilot:** 3 separate scripts (header.min.js, store settings file, widget bootstrap). That is 3 HTTP requests plus JS execution for a review widget. If Trustpilot reviews are not prominently displayed above the fold, these scripts could be deferred or loaded only when the review section scrolls into view.
- **Snapchat Pixel:** Fires on page load via intg.snapchat.com.
- **Klaviyo:** Initializes on page load for email capture and popup functionality.
- **Instafeed (nfcube.com):** Loads an Instagram feed widget. If this appears below the fold (likely on the homepage), it should be lazy-loaded rather than firing immediately.
- **Kiwi Sizing:** The sizing plugin loads on every page, but is only needed on PDPs where the size selector is present. Loading it sitewide adds unnecessary weight to the homepage and collection pages.
- **Microsoft Clarity:** Session recording and heatmap tool (visible in Shopify admin). Another tracking script executing on page load.
- **Google Analytics (GA4):** Standard analytics tracking.
- **hCaptcha:** Security script for forms.
- **Shop Pay / Portable Wallets:** Payment module scripts.

Each of these scripts adds to the Total Blocking Time and competes for browser resources during the critical first render. The compounding effect of all of them firing simultaneously is likely a major contributor to the 760ms TBT and 3.1s FCP.

**2. Recommendations (verified)**

- **Defer Trustpilot scripts:** If Trustpilot widgets are not visible in the first viewport, load them on scroll or user interaction instead of on page load. Three scripts for one widget is heavy.
- **Defer Instafeed:** The Instagram feed is almost certainly below the fold. Lazy-load it when the user scrolls near it.
- **Conditionally load Kiwi Sizing:** Only load the sizing plugin on PDP pages where the size selector exists. Remove it from the homepage and collection pages.
- **Audit Snapchat pixel necessity:** If Snapchat is not a significant traffic or conversion channel (it does not appear in the Shopify referrer data), consider removing the pixel or deferring it.
- **Evaluate Microsoft Clarity usage:** If the team is not actively using Clarity session recordings, removing it eliminates another tracking script from every page load.

**3. Double Overlay on First Load**

The 10% off email capture popup and the cookie consent banner both fire immediately on page load. This creates two full-screen or partial-screen interruptions before the visitor has seen any content. For a luxury brand, this is a poor first impression. Consider:
- Delaying the 10% off popup by 5-10 seconds or triggering it on scroll/exit intent
- Ensuring the cookie consent and popup do not stack simultaneously

## Winning Tests Library (Convertibles Case Studies)

Relevant case studies fetched from convertibles.dev:

**1. Homepage Storytelling Module (luxury pajama brand)**
- Added an "Experience" brand story module above reviews on homepage
- WIN: +$130,251/month
- Key learning: "Story before price changes the frame. When visitors understand the craft first, the price makes sense." Specificity (mentioning the studio location) builds trust. Sequence matters: brand story should precede social proof.

**2. Press Logo Bar with Editorial Quotes (gin subscription brand)**
- Added rotating press quotes above an expanded "As Seen In" logo bar
- WIN on desktop: +$9,392/month. LOSS on mobile.
- Key learning: Device-specific performance matters. Press quotes won on desktop where users read more, lost on mobile where vertical space is constrained. Must test separately by device.

**3. Collection Page Hero + Reviews + Urgency (slime brand)**
- Progressive test: hero banner + star ratings on product cards + "LIMITED STOCK" labels
- WIN: +$114,870/month
- Key learning: Multiple trust signals compound. The hero banner established context, reviews built credibility, and scarcity labels motivated action. Each layer contributed incrementally.

**4. Homepage Banner Copy (VR gunstock brand)**
- Tested bold product claim ("THE BEST DAMN VR GUNSTOCK ON THE PLANET") vs. generic benefits
- WIN: +$19,441/month
- Key learning: Clarity outperforms cleverness. Confident claims backed by proof beat vague benefit language.

**5. Instagram Stories Social Proof on PDP (VR brand)**
- Instagram-style circular story bubble UI with real customer photos above CTA
- WIN: +$6,635/month
- Key learning: Familiar UI patterns reduce cognitive friction. Showing people using the product is more persuasive than product shots alone. Placing social proof where hesitation occurs maximizes impact.

**6. Collection Page ATF Optimization (sensory brand)**
- Tested lifestyle banner + benefit-driven copy + consolidated filters
- WIN: +$13,730/month
- Key learning: Emotional messaging outperformed product-only imagery. Consolidated filters improved product visibility above the fold.

**7. Product Description Copy Angles (dog treats brand)**
- Tested customer-voice descriptions emphasizing specific benefits vs. generic brand copy
- WIN: +$50,945/month with the duration/longevity angle
- Key learning: Specificity and broad appeal win. Customer language resonates more than brand-created copy. Quantified claims beat vague marketing language.

## Cross-Source Analysis: Converging Themes

### Theme 1: The Trust Gap (5 sources converge)

The site asks visitors to spend £649-1,865 on a product from a brand they may not know, with zero trust signals visible. Shopify shows 98.83% of sessions end without an add-to-cart. GA4 shows the homepage (33% of all users) holds attention for only 8 seconds. The homepage has no reviews, no press, no brand story, no USPs. PDPs show price and photos but no social proof, no craftsmanship story, no customer validation. Yet Trustpilot reviews (4.5 stars, 112 reviews) are overwhelmingly positive, and repeat customers describe the products as "worth every penny." The trust exists in the customer base. It just does not exist on the website.

### Theme 2: The Story-to-Site Disconnect (4 sources converge)

Bocan's emails, ads, and in-person experience all tell a compelling brand story: handmade in London, 100% organic Aegean cotton, bespoke customizations, personal shopping with Ilayda, boutiques in London and Istanbul. But the website strips all of this away. Emails describe "slow mornings, maternity ease, and quiet evenings." PDPs show a photo and a price. Ads mention "Designed in London." The homepage does not. The WhatsApp experience, where Ilayda personally guides customers through customization, is the brand's best sales channel. The website captures none of this magic.

### Theme 3: Mobile Experience Failure (3 sources converge)

80% of traffic is mobile. Page speed scores 45/100 with a 5.9s LCP (nearly 6 seconds to see main content). The mobile homepage loads with two overlays (10% off popup + cookie consent) before any content is visible. At £1,689 AOV on a 5.9s mobile load time, the experience is actively working against conversion.

### Theme 4: Collection Page Decision Paralysis (2 sources converge)

The Maternity Edit has 126 products for what's supposed to be a "curated edit." The Eid Sale has 63 products. The Angels' Dream collection has 59. These pages have minimal context, no social proof, and limited curation. When an email or ad promises a curated selection, the landing page dumps visitors into an overwhelming grid.

### Theme 5: Hidden Differentiators (3 sources converge)

Made-To-Measure, private personal shopping, bespoke customizations, expert alterations, complimentary luxury gift wrapping, boutiques in London and Istanbul, free international shipping, handcrafted production. All of these exist but are either invisible on the site or mentioned only in emails. For a brand competing against Olivia von Halle with zero press coverage, these operational differentiators need to be front and center, not hidden behind email newsletters.
