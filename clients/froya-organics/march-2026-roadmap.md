## Test #1: Landing Page Hero - Message Match & Trust Compression

**Page:** Main Landing Page ([/pages/fr-ya-organics-hyper-potent-arctic-balms-for-mature-skin](https://froyaorganics.com/pages/fr-ya-organics-hyper-potent-arctic-balms-for-mature-skin)). 1,216,799 sessions in the last 30 days.

**Hypothesis:** If we align the hero section with the ad messaging and compress trust signals above the fold, add-to-cart rate will increase because the current hero ("Delay Wrinkles by 10 years, Guaranteed") doesn't match what any of the 6 top ads promise, and the trust elements that address the top purchase objections are buried 6+ scrolls deep on mobile.

**Data:** All 6 top-performing Meta ads lead with the Norse/Arctic origin story, customer testimonials, and the 60-day guarantee, but the LP hero uses a different framing entirely (Source: Meta Ads). 9.2% of buyers cited trust/legitimacy and 5.3% cited AI/ads concerns as what almost stopped them, with verbatims like "afraid of a scam," "not sure if people in ads were AI generated," and "too good to be true" (Source: Survey, 6,584 responses). The 60-day guarantee appears only at the bottom of the page despite being the #1 trust signal in every ad (Source: Site Audit). Press logos (TeenVogue, Cosmopolitan, marie claire) sit below the fold on mobile where 91.5% of traffic lands (Source: Shopify Analytics). A similar brand storytelling + trust compression test drove +$130K/month for a luxury brand with price as the #1 objection (Source: Winning Tests Library).

**V1:** Replace the current hero headline with copy that mirrors the top ad hook: lead with the Norse/Arctic origin story and the 60-day guarantee. Keep the existing hero image. Add a compressed trust bar directly below the headline: star rating + review count + customer count + guarantee badge, all on one line (e.g., "4,478 Reviews | 176K+ Women | 60-Day Guarantee"). Move the press logos from below the fold to a single-line bar directly under the trust bar. The hero CTA stays in the same position. Everything below the hero section remains unchanged.
**Brief:** Design: new hero headline text (2 lines max), a single-row trust bar with 3 data points separated by dividers, and a compact press logo strip. Mobile: headline stacks above hero image, trust bar sits between headline and CTA as a full-width strip, press logos in a horizontal scroll below CTA. Desktop: headline overlays or sits beside hero image, trust bar inline below headline, press logos in a static row. Dev: text and layout changes only within the existing hero section. No new components. No structural changes to sections below the hero.

**V2:** All V1 changes, plus add a 3-block objection handler between the hero and the "Before & Afters" section. Each block is one sentence answering the top 3 objections from the survey data. Block 1, "Will it work?": lead with the clinical trial stat (currently buried mid-page in the "Scientists Shocked by Results" section). Block 2, "Is it worth the price?": per-item value breakdown of the 4-product system showing total value vs. subscription price. Block 3, "Can I trust this brand?": "176K+ women, 4,478 verified reviews, 60-day money-back guarantee." Compact, scannable, no more than 3 lines per block.
**Brief:** Design: V1 hero changes plus a new 3-column section (desktop) / 3-row stack (mobile) between the hero and the existing "Before & Afters" section. Each block has a bold one-line question header and 2-3 lines of answer text. Mobile: blocks stack vertically, each with a left-aligned icon, full-width, minimal padding. Desktop: three equal columns with centered text. Dev: new section inserted below hero, above existing "Before & Afters" section. Static content. Clinical trial stat, product values, and review count are hardcoded (not dynamic). No changes to any other page sections.

## Test #2: PDP Buy Box - Value Framing & Decision-Point Trust

**Page:** The Complete System for Mature Women's Skin PDP ([/products/the-complete-system-for-mature-women-s-skin](https://froyaorganics.com/products/the-complete-system-for-mature-women-s-skin)). 212,994 sessions in the last 30 days, $4,470,000 in revenue.

**Hypothesis:** If we restructure the buy box to show per-item value and surface trust signals at the point of purchase, conversion rate will increase because 52.9% of buyers say price almost stopped them, the current PDP shows a single system price ($159) with no breakdown of what each product is worth individually, and the subscription option is positioned secondary to one-time purchase despite offering 40% savings.

**Data:** Price is the #1 objection at 52.9% of survey respondents, with verbatims like "I didn't understand which product I needed most so opted for a set but that's quite pricey" and "the bundle comes across a bit like a hard sell" (Source: Survey). The PDP shows $159 for the system but never communicates the per-item value, making it impossible for visitors to assess whether it's a good deal (Source: Site Audit). The subscription option ("SAVE 40% ON YOUR 1ST ORDER") sits below the one-time purchase option and partially below the fold on mobile (Source: Site Audit). A subscription value breakdown test showing individual item RRPs drove +$41K/month (Source: Winning Tests Library). A PDP buy box restructure grouping price + reviews + active CTAs drove +$386K/month for a luxury brand (Source: Winning Tests Library). Competitors like True Botanicals and Pai Skincare show clear per-product pricing and prominent subscription savings (Source: Competitor Research).

**V1:** Restructure the buy box pricing section. Add a per-item value breakdown directly below the "System Includes" list: show each product's individual retail price (e.g., "Anti-Age & Insane Glow Day Balm: $65 value / Hyper Potent Dark Circles & Eye Balm: $48 value / Magic Wrinkle Eraser Night Balm: $52 value / Ultra Cleanse & Revive Face Scrub: $35 value"). Add a "Total value: $200" line, then show the system price. Flip the pricing option order: show the subscription option first (highlighted as recommended) with the one-time purchase below it. Add a "60-Day Guarantee" badge directly next to the ATC button. Everything else in the buy box (star rating, review count, 176K+ women, benefits icons, product image) stays unchanged.
**Brief:** Design: value breakdown as a simple list with right-aligned dollar amounts below the existing system contents list. Bold "Total value: $200" summary line. Subscription option gets a highlighted border or background color, positioned above one-time purchase. Small guarantee badge (icon + text) inline to the left of or directly below the ATC button. Mobile: value breakdown is full-width, each item on one line. Subscription/one-time toggle stacks vertically, subscription on top. Guarantee badge full-width below ATC. Desktop: value breakdown sits within the existing buy box column. Subscription/one-time options side by side or stacked with subscription highlighted. Guarantee badge inline next to ATC. Dev: reorder existing pricing elements (subscription above one-time). Add static value breakdown text. Add guarantee badge image/SVG. No changes to product gallery, description, or page sections below the buy box.

**V2:** All V1 changes, plus add 3 mini customer testimonials between the benefits icons and the pricing options. Each testimonial is one sentence from a verified buyer addressing a key concern: one about results ("My wrinkles visibly reduced in weeks"), one about value ("I've spent $2000+ on other products, Froya is the first to deliver"), one about the subscription ("Love that I can pause or cancel anytime"). Each includes a first name and a verified badge. These sit right at the decision point, between the "why buy" (benefits) and the "how to buy" (pricing).
**Brief:** Design: 3 compact testimonial cards in a horizontal scroll (mobile) or side-by-side row (desktop), each with a quote, first name, and a small verified badge icon. Positioned between the benefits icons row and the pricing options. Mobile: horizontal scroll with snap-to-card behavior, cards are ~80% viewport width. Desktop: three equal-width cards in a row. Dev: static HTML/CSS component. Testimonial content is hardcoded. Positioned between the existing benefits icons section and the pricing toggle section. No changes to other buy box elements.

## Test #3: Landing Page - Objection-Led Social Proof Section

**Page:** Main Landing Page ([/pages/fr-ya-organics-hyper-potent-arctic-balms-for-mature-skin](https://froyaorganics.com/pages/fr-ya-organics-hyper-potent-arctic-balms-for-mature-skin)). 1,216,799 sessions in the last 30 days.

**Scheduling:** Must run after Test #1 completes (both target the main LP). Minimum 2-week gap.

**Hypothesis:** If we replace the current mid-page testimonials section with an objection-led social proof block positioned higher on the page, scroll-to-product rate and ATC will increase because the current "Real customers, real results" section is buried deep (8+ scrolls on mobile), and 9.2% trust + 8.4% "will it work?" + 5.3% AI/ads concerns represent 23% of buyers who needed convincing before purchasing.

**Data:** The current customer testimonials sit deep on the page after multiple education sections, by which point most mobile visitors have dropped off (Source: Site Audit). 9.2% cited trust concerns, 8.4% questioned whether the product works, and 5.3% worried about AI-generated content in ads, totaling 23% of respondents who needed proof of authenticity (Source: Survey, 6,584 responses). Verbatims include "I've tried numerous products and none of them improve my aging skin," "too good to be true," and "not sure if the people talking in your ads were AI generated" (Source: Survey). Competitors like True Botanicals use user-submitted before/after photos with verification badges, while Pai Skincare has an explicit unretouched imagery policy (Source: Competitor Research). An Instagram-Stories-style UGC element drove +$6.6K/month for a mobile-heavy brand by placing real customer photos at the decision point (Source: Winning Tests Library). A checkout social proof test using named testimonials with profile images drove +$6.1K/month (Source: Winning Tests Library).

**V1:** Move the "Real customers, real results" section from its current deep position to directly below the "What you should expect from using Froya Daily" timeline. Replace the current testimonial format with 6 customer before/after photo pairs, each with a first name, age, time using the product (e.g., "Sarah, 54, 6 weeks"), and one sentence in their own words. Add a "Verified Purchase" badge to each. Remove any imagery that could be perceived as stock or AI-generated. The existing testimonial section in its original deep position is removed to avoid duplication. All other page sections remain in their current order.
**Brief:** Design: 6 before/after cards in a 2-column grid (desktop) or vertical scroll (mobile). Each card shows side-by-side before/after photos, name + age + duration below, one-line quote, and a small "Verified Purchase" badge. Mobile: cards stack vertically or use a horizontal snap-scroll carousel. Each card is full-width in stack mode. Desktop: 2x3 grid with consistent card sizing. Dev: new section positioned after the "What you should expect" timeline, replacing the testimonial section from its current deeper location. Requires sourcing 6 real customer before/after photos with permission. Static content. If Test #1 V2 is the winner, the 3-block objection handler sits between the hero and "Before & Afters," and this section sits below the "What you should expect" timeline (no conflict).

**V2:** All V1 changes, plus add a "What almost stopped our customers" callout block directly above the before/after photos. This uses real anonymized survey data: show the top 3 objections as quotes ("The price almost stopped me," "I was afraid it was a scam," "I wasn't sure it would work on my skin") each paired with a one-line resolution from a real customer who had that same concern and converted (e.g., "...then I saw results in 2 weeks and subscribed immediately"). This creates a narrative arc: objection, resolution, proof (the before/afters below).
**Brief:** Design: 3 objection-resolution pairs in a vertical stack. Each pair has the objection in a muted/lighter style (like a quote bubble) and the resolution in bold or highlighted text below it. Sits directly above the V1 before/after grid. Mobile: full-width stack, each pair separated by subtle dividers. Desktop: 3-column layout or vertical stack depending on available width. Dev: static HTML/CSS. Content is hardcoded from survey data (anonymized). Positioned as the intro to the before/after section. No dynamic elements.

## Test #4: Cart Drawer - Price Transparency & Trust Stack

**Page:** Cart drawer (site-wide). Affects all 136,760 monthly ATC visitors across every page and product.

**Hypothesis:** If we show the full order total on the checkout button and stack trust signals above it, checkout completion rate will increase because 27.5% of visitors who add to cart never reach checkout and 52% who reach checkout don't purchase, while 52.9% of buyers say price almost stopped them and 3.8% cite shipping costs as an objection. Surprise charges at checkout are a known conversion killer.

**Data:** The funnel shows 136,760 ATC visitors in the last 30 days, but only 99,220 reach checkout (27.5% drop-off) and only 47,632 complete purchase (52% drop-off from checkout) (Source: Shopify Analytics). Shipping costs are a named objection for 3.8% of survey respondents, with verbatims like "delivery charge was quite high" and "shipping costs" (Source: Survey). Price is the #1 objection at 52.9%, and the current cart drawer does not reinforce value or address hesitation at the point of commitment (Source: Site Audit). A cart drawer checkout button showing the full total with a lock icon drove +$389K/month for a luxury brand with a similar AOV. That was the single largest revenue lift in the entire case study library (Source: Winning Tests Library). Separately, stacking three trust signals (review count + customer count + returns policy) above the checkout button in the cart drawer drove +$32K/month for a wellness brand (Source: Winning Tests Library).

**V1:** Replace the current checkout button text with a lock icon + "CHECKOUT - $[TOTAL]" showing the full order total including shipping and taxes. Above the checkout button, add a single-line trust stack: "4,478 Reviews | 176K+ Customers | 60-Day Money Back." Below the cart items, show the savings amount in dollars if a subscription is selected (e.g., "You're saving $41 with Subscribe & Save"). Everything else in the cart drawer (product thumbnails, quantity selectors, upsells if any) stays unchanged.
**Brief:** Design: checkout button with lock icon left-aligned, "CHECKOUT" centered, and dynamic dollar total right-aligned. Trust stack is a single line of small text with dividers, centered above the button. Savings line in green text below cart items. Mobile: full-width sticky checkout button at bottom of cart drawer, trust stack above it. Desktop: same layout within the cart drawer panel. Dev: dynamic total calculation including shipping estimate and taxes. If shipping is free, show "CHECKOUT - $[SUBTOTAL] (Free Shipping)." Savings calculation pulls from the subscription vs. one-time price delta. Trust stack is static text. No changes to cart item display or upsell elements.

**V2:** All V1 changes, plus add a mini subscription value callout inside the cart drawer for non-subscription items. If the customer has a one-time purchase in cart, show a subtle banner: "Switch to Subscribe & Save: get this order for $[SUBSCRIPTION PRICE] instead of $[ONE-TIME PRICE]. Cancel anytime." Include a one-tap toggle to switch the item to subscription directly in the cart. This addresses the 7.8% subscription objection by framing subscription as savings with explicit cancel-anytime reassurance at the final decision point.
**Brief:** Design: subscription callout as a soft-colored banner between the cart items and the trust stack. Contains the price comparison, a "Switch to Subscribe" toggle or button, and "Cancel anytime" in small text. Mobile: full-width banner, toggle is a simple tap target. Desktop: same layout within cart drawer. Dev: dynamic pricing comparison pulling one-time vs. subscription price for each product. Toggle triggers a cart update via AJAX (no page reload) to switch the line item to subscription variant. If item is already subscription, hide the banner. Works with Loop Subscriptions integration. V1 changes (checkout total, trust stack, savings) remain.

## Test #5: Landing Page - Condensed Page Structure

**Page:** Main Landing Page ([/pages/fr-ya-organics-hyper-potent-arctic-balms-for-mature-skin](https://froyaorganics.com/pages/fr-ya-organics-hyper-potent-arctic-balms-for-mature-skin)). 1,216,799 sessions in the last 30 days.

**Scheduling:** Must run after Tests #1 and #3 complete (same page). This is a structural test that supersedes section-level changes.

**Hypothesis:** If we cut the landing page from 10+ sections to 5 core sections and bring the product offer within 3 scrolls on mobile, conversion rate will increase because 91.5% of traffic is mobile, the current page requires 6+ scrolls to reach the first product/pricing section, and survey data shows visitors find the site "messy," "confusing," and "too busy." Most visitors arriving from Meta ads are pre-sold on the concept. They need a clear path to purchase, not 10 sections of education.

**Data:** 91.5% of sessions are mobile, where the current LP requires extensive scrolling through hero, before/afters, expectations timeline, clinical trial section, and brand story before reaching the first product/pricing module (Source: Shopify Analytics, Site Audit). 3.2% of survey respondents cite website/UX issues, with verbatims like "the website is quite messy with the discounts and subscription options," "too many offers and special prices, so confusing," "prices are so vague and change across the website, very American-style too busy website," and "too much going on on a site, not easy to navigate" (Source: Survey). The "11 reasons why arctic herbs beat retinol" page grew +1,200% MoM to 81K sessions, suggesting shorter, focused content pages convert well (Source: Shopify Analytics). All 6 top Meta ads pre-sell the origin story, ingredients, guarantee, and results before the visitor even lands. Repeating all of this in 10 sections creates redundancy (Source: Meta Ads). A homepage CTA copy test showed that benefit-first messaging with a secondary "How it works" link outperformed pages that front-loaded education (Source: Winning Tests Library).

**V1:** Reduce the landing page to 5 sections in this order: (1) Hero with ad-matched headline, trust bar, and CTA (as per Test #1 winner or current hero if #1 hasn't run). (2) Before/after results (3 pairs, not the current full gallery). (3) The Complete System product module with pricing, subscription toggle, per-item value breakdown, and ATC button. (4) "Real customers, real results" with 4 short testimonials. (5) 60-day guarantee + FAQ accordion. Remove: the expectations timeline, "Scientists Shocked by Results" section, "Diet for Line" section, "Why Arctic Plants Are Incredibly Potent" section, and the long-form brand story. The product offer moves from scroll 6+ to scroll 3 on mobile. Desktop: same 5-section structure.
**Brief:** Design: 5-section page layout. Each section is self-contained. Hero reuses existing hero assets. Before/after section is a 3-card horizontal scroll (mobile) or row (desktop). Product module mirrors the PDP buy box (image left, details right on desktop; stacked on mobile). Testimonials are a 4-card grid (desktop) or vertical stack (mobile). Guarantee + FAQ at the bottom. Mobile: total page length should be approximately half the current page. Desktop: proportionally shorter. Dev: rebuild the page template with 5 sections. Can reuse existing section Liquid templates or create a new streamlined template. Assets (images, copy, pricing) pulled from existing product data. ATC button triggers the same cart drawer. This is a significant structural change. QA on both mobile and desktop required.

**V2:** All V1 changes, plus add a sticky bottom bar on mobile that appears after the user scrolls past the hero. The bar shows: product name, subscription price, and an "Add to Cart" button. This ensures the purchase option is always accessible regardless of scroll position. The bar disappears when the user reaches the product module section (to avoid duplication) and reappears after they scroll past it.
**Brief:** Design: sticky bar is 56-64px tall, sits at the bottom of the mobile viewport. Contains product name (truncated if needed), price in bold, and a compact ATC button in the brand orange. Semi-transparent background with a subtle top border. Bar animates in (slide up) when user scrolls past hero, fades out when the product module is in viewport, reappears when product module scrolls out of view. Desktop: no sticky bar (not needed with shorter page and persistent sidebar layout). Dev: JavaScript scroll listener to toggle visibility based on the product module's position in the viewport. ATC button triggers the same cart drawer as the main ATC. Uses IntersectionObserver for performance. Z-index above page content but below modals/drawers.

## Test #6: Site-Wide Popup - Free Gift Bundle vs. Percentage Discount

**Page:** Site-wide popup. Affects all 2,926,198 monthly sessions.

**Hypothesis:** If we replace the current percentage-based discount popup with a free gift bundle offer, email capture rate and AOV will both increase because free tangible gifts create stronger perceived value than abstract percentage savings, discounts are already growing +52% MoM (eroding margin), and 15.9% of surveyed customers specifically asked for samples/trial sizes as their ideal gift.

**Data:** Discounts grew +52% MoM to $347,152 in the last 30 days, while net sales grew only +18%, meaning discount growth is outpacing revenue growth by nearly 3x (Source: Shopify Analytics). 52.9% of survey respondents cite price as their top objection, but the current approach of offering percentage discounts trains customers to wait for deals rather than perceiving product value (Source: Survey). When asked what surprise gift they'd most want, 15.9% said samples/trial sizes, 12.4% said eye/dark circle products, and 10.1% said hair products, revealing specific demand for product discovery (Source: Survey, 5,738 gift responses). A free gift bundle test (3 gifts with $60 minimum) drove +$60K/month, +26% email capture, and +25% AOV, outperforming percentage discounts, dollar-off discounts, and single free gifts (Source: Winning Tests Library). A separate test found $30-off with a minimum spend outperformed 15% off by +$45K/month and +21% AOV (Source: Winning Tests Library).

**V1:** Replace the current welcome popup offer with a free gift bundle: "Get 3 Free Gifts With Your First Order (min. $99)." The gifts should be trial-size products from the most-requested categories: a mini Eye Balm, a mini Day Balm sample, and a trial-size product from the hair or body range. Show product imagery of the 3 gifts in the popup. Keep the email capture field. The minimum spend threshold of $99 sits just below the current AOV ($100.07), ensuring most orders qualify while nudging low-AOV orders upward. The popup triggers on the same existing conditions (timing, page views, etc.).
**Brief:** Design: popup with headline ("3 Free Gifts With Your First Order"), product images of the 3 trial-size items arranged horizontally, brief description of each gift, "$99 minimum" in small text, email input field, and submit CTA. Mobile: popup takes ~70% of viewport height, product images stack or display in a compact row, email field and CTA at the bottom. Desktop: centered modal, product images in a row, email field below. Dev: replace existing popup offer logic. Email capture flows to the same ESP. Discount code auto-applies a free gift bundle (set up as a Shopify automatic discount or script) when cart meets the $99 threshold. Requires creating 3 trial-size product variants or gift SKUs in Shopify if they don't already exist. Free gifts should be added to cart automatically at checkout, not require manual selection.

**V2:** All V1 changes, but test a tiered gift structure instead of a flat bundle. Tier 1 ($79+): 1 free gift (mini Eye Balm). Tier 2 ($119+): 2 free gifts (mini Eye Balm + mini Day Balm). Tier 3 ($159+): 3 free gifts (all three). Show all 3 tiers in the popup so the customer sees what they unlock at higher spend levels. Add a progress bar in the cart drawer showing how close the customer is to the next gift tier (e.g., "Add $40 more to unlock your 2nd free gift"). This creates a natural upsell mechanism tied to the gift structure.
**Brief:** Design: popup shows 3 tiers with product images and spend thresholds. Each tier is a row: spend amount on the left, gift(s) on the right, with the current tier highlighted. Mobile: tiers stack vertically. Desktop: tiers in a compact table or card layout. Cart drawer progress bar sits above the cart items: a horizontal bar showing current subtotal vs. next tier threshold, with the unlocked gift(s) displayed. Mobile: full-width bar at the top of the cart drawer. Desktop: same position. Dev: tiered discount logic in Shopify (3 automatic discounts with different thresholds, or a script). Cart drawer progress bar requires JavaScript to calculate distance to next tier and update dynamically as items are added/removed. Free gifts auto-add when tier thresholds are met and auto-remove if the customer drops below the threshold. Email capture in the popup flows to the same ESP regardless of tier.
